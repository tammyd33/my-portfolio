Color Theory

Color theory is the study of how colors interact with each other and how they affect peopleâ€™s emotions and perceptions. Colors can draw attention, make you feel a certain way, and impact your design's overall message. Below are some common terms related to color theory.

Term Definition
Color Wheel a fundamental tool in color theory which illustrates the relationship between colors
Primary Colors colors that are pure and cannot be created by mixing other colors: red, blue, and yellow
Secondary Colors colors that are formed by mixing two primary colors: green, violet, and orange
Tertiary Colors colors that are a result of mixing a primary color with a secondary color: blue-violet, blue-green, yellow-green, yellow-orange, red-orange, and red-violet
Saturation the degree of purity and brightness within a color
Hue the purest form of a color
Shade a hue with black added to darken the color
Tint a hue with white added to lighten the color
Tone a hue with grey added to give a muted color
Value of Hue the relative lightness or darkness of a color
Color Scheme a set of colors, properly labeled and made available to everyone involved with a project in order to ensure a cohesive look and feel
Monochromatic Colors Scheme a color palette that uses shades and tints of a single color to created a unified look
Triadic Color Scheme a color palette that uses three colors that are evenly spaced around the color wheel to form a triangle
Complementary Color Scheme a color palette that uses colors that are opposite each other on the color wheel
Split Color Scheme a color palette that uses a base color and two colors that would be balanced from the opposite side of the color wheel
Analogous Color Scheme colors that are next to each other on the color wheel
White Space the empty space between and around design elements and text, also called negative space, that can be any color, texture, or pattern
RGB stands for red, green, and blue and it is a color mode used by screens to build a light-based color system
CMYK stands for cyan, magenta, yellow, and black and are colored inks used in the printing process
Contrast the difference between the lightest and darkest tones in an image
Vibrancy in color theory this means a lively, bright, rich, energetic hue
